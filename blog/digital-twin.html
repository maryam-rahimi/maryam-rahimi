Imaging Transcriptomics: Connecting Molecular Signals to Brain Function

Published: December 2025 | Author: Maryam Rahimi Movassagh

One of the most exciting shifts in modern neuroscience is the emergence of imaging transcriptomics—a field that integrates brain imaging with gene-expression data to uncover how molecular processes shape cognition, behavior, and disease. For decades, neuroscience and molecular biology evolved as separate disciplines. Today, multi-omics technologies allow us to map gene activity at the single-cell level, while imaging tools such as MRI and PET reveal functional and structural patterns across the brain.

My Ph.D. research aims to bridge these two worlds using interpretable AI frameworks, connecting cell-level signals to large-scale neural activity. This integration is crucial for understanding disorders such as ALS, epilepsy, and Alzheimer’s disease, where molecular disturbances precede functional decline.

Imaging transcriptomics provides a new lens for understanding the brain: it links what the brain looks like with what the brain is made of.

Why Imaging Transcriptomics Matters

Traditional imaging reveals how the brain behaves—its networks, rhythms, and structural organization. But imaging alone cannot tell us why these patterns emerge. Transcriptomics, on the other hand, captures gene-expression activity across brain regions, grounding functional signals in molecular biology.

When combined, the result is a powerful multimodal system capable of:

  • Identifying molecular drivers behind imaging abnormalities
  • Detecting early biomarkers of neurodegeneration
  • Understanding how genes influence cognition and behavior
  • Predicting disease progression through multi-scale modeling

Figure: Imaging transcriptomics links brain activity with the underlying molecular architecture.

Building AI Models That Learn Across Scales

My research develops AI systems that combine:

  • Single-cell RNA sequencing
  • Spatial gene-expression gradients
  • Neuroimaging modalities (EEG, fMRI, diffusion MRI)
  • Clinical and behavioral measurements

To integrate these diverse data sources, I leverage graph neural networks (GNNs), foundation models, and interpretable statistical learning to map cross-scale relationships. These models identify which molecular pathways correspond to observed neural dysfunction—helping us understand how diseases emerge and progress.

“Which genes shape the brain’s vulnerability to degeneration, and how early can we detect those effects?”

Answering questions like this requires models that are not only powerful, but transparent—tools that clinicians and neuroscientists can interpret and trust.

Toward Precision Neuroscience

Imaging transcriptomics is a cornerstone in the journey toward precision neuroscience. By connecting molecular and systems-level data, we unlock new opportunities for:

  • Early diagnosis of neurological disorders
  • Developing personalized treatments
  • Improving drug response predictions
  • Understanding the molecular architecture of cognition

Through multi-omics integration and interpretable AI, we can finally begin to decode how the brain’s biology drives its function—moving closer to truly personalized brain health.
