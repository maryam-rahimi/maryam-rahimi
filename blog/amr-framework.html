Understanding the Brain Through Single-Cell and Multi-Omics AI

Published: December 2025 | Author: Maryam Rahimi Movassagh

Neuroscience is undergoing a data revolution. For the first time in history, we can measure the brain not only at the systems level—through MRI and EEG— but also at the molecular level, through single-cell sequencing, multi-omics profiling, and spatial transcriptomics. Yet with this abundance of data comes a challenge: how do we connect what happens inside individual cells to large-scale brain function and neurological disorders?

My Ph.D. research focuses on building interpretable AI frameworks that bridge molecular-level signals with systems-level behavior. By integrating single-cell data, transcriptomics, and neuroimaging into unified computational models, I aim to uncover early biomarkers and mechanistic patterns underlying conditions such as ALS, epilepsy, and Alzheimer's disease.

Modern neuroscience requires models that do more than predict. They must explain—revealing how cellular and molecular changes cascade into cognitive and behavioral outcomes.

Why Single-Cell and Multi-Omics Matter

Traditional genomic studies treat tissues as homogeneous, averaging signals across thousands of cells. Single-cell technologies flip this assumption by allowing us to see what each cell is doing individually—its gene expression, its state, and its developmental trajectory.

When combined with proteomics, epigenomics, metabolomics, and brain imaging, these datasets give us a panoramic view of the nervous system. But such high-dimensional data require AI systems that can:

  • Model interactions across biological scales
  • Detect subtle molecular signatures of disease
  • Infer causal relationships rather than correlations
  • Remain interpretable for clinicians and neuroscientists

Figure: Multi-omics and imaging provide complementary layers of brain information, from genes to circuits to cognition.

Building Interpretable AI for Neuroscience

In my work, I design AI systems that merge graph neural networks, foundation models, and probabilistic reasoning to represent the brain as both a molecular and functional network.

These models learn from:

  • Single-cell transcriptomes
  • Spatial gene-expression gradients
  • EEG and fMRI dynamics
  • Clinical progression patterns

The ultimate goal is to create systems that can answer questions such as:

“Which gene-expression patterns predict the transition from healthy neural activity to early disease states?”

These insights could support earlier diagnosis, help evaluate treatment effectiveness, and guide biomedical discovery toward more personalized interventions.

Why This Research Matters

Neurological disorders remain some of the least understood and most challenging diseases in medicine. By connecting molecular omics with systems-level brain function through interpretable AI, we move closer to:

  • Early detection of neurodegenerative diseases
  • Targeted therapeutic strategies
  • Better understanding of cognitive decline
  • Precision medicine tailored to individuals

As neuroscience becomes increasingly multi-modal, the need for transparent, trustworthy, and biologically grounded AI will only grow. My research aims to contribute to this shift— building computational tools that help decode the brain not as a single system, but as an interconnected biological universe.
